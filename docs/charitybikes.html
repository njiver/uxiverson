Charity Bikes

Product Design | Supporting a children's orphanage in Ho Chi Minh City

Overview

I aquired two secondhand 100cc motorcycles with a friend, dismantled them and studied how to rewire the electrical system. After creating an electrical diagram and rewiring the bikes I then tuned, detailed them, and designed custom decals. Funds from thier sale supported the purchase of 1 monthâ€™s rice for 50 children at an orphanage in Ho Chi Minh City.


The Bikes

Dismantling the Bikes

These bikes are the backpacker's bike of choice for riding across Vietnam. The Win 110cc is cheap, a blast to ride and easy to find parts for but generally in disrepair. With all the bikes I'd taken around the country many were on thier last legs and break downs were imminent. Most are a copy of a Honda design that was discontinued in the early 2000's. I'd had much experience riding them but not much experience repairing them beyond quick fixes. As I have always had electrical issues with these bikes I wanted to learn how to rewire them and bring them back into proper running order.

Diagraming the Wiring

Without a repair manual in hand I researched wiring online and sketched out the connections to various parts. I found that the wiring of the bikes were more similar to a scooter wiring plan than to a standard motorcycle wiring plan. I updated my circuit diagram in Illustrator to make it easier to follow.

Updated wiring diagram

Sketch of wiring

Parts and Detailing

I had many trips back and forth to Dan Sinh tool market looking for the right vendor the right tools. I picked up sockets, wrenches, pliers, wire strippers, crimpers, connectors, 16-gauge wire, and heat shrink tubing. I found that parts and tools at Tan Hung market were extremely overpriced so I turned to my local Sua Xe (repairman) to purchase parts from. I temporarily swapped the old air intakes, plastic boxes with horrible filters, with filter filled beer cans. Suprisingly the bikes still ran great.


Looking for parts


Giving the Bikes Some Style

The Beer Bikes

With the beer can air intakes on, and the bikes still running, the idea for the beer bikes was born. Considering that everything from giant ice cubes to beer is delivered on bikes in Vietnam I decided to start designing decals based on two popular brands, Bia Saigon and Bia 333. I created a stencil from the gas tank and ferrings then imported the shapes to design from in Illustrator. I created mockups to explain the idea to the decal shop.







Applying the Decals

I took the bikes to Toan Decal shop on Nguyen Chi Thanh to have the designs printed and applied. Within an hour the bikes were ready to go.

Take Notes, Dig In, and Make It Meaningful!

My notes saved me when it came to the wiring. There's no way I'd be able to remember and check each connection without diagramming. Photos helped quite a bit as well. It took me about 10 weeks to tackle this project from end to end but it motivated me to challenge myself to do greater things.

I decided to donate the funds from the sale of the bikes to an orphanage in the city. The last thing that I learned amazed me. For the value of these old bikes the orphanage purchased enough rice to feed 50 children for 1 month.
